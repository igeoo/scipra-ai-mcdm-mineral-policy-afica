If Xᵢ = μ for all i, then (Xᵢ − μ) = 0 for all i, so Var(X) = 0 and σ(X) = 0. By Equation (A.10):
RPCI  =  max(0, μ − 0) / (1 + λ/2)  =  μ / (1 + λ/2)  <  μ  =  PCI₀  for λ > 0. 
Interpretation: Even at perfect cross-domain balance (σ = 0), RPCI is discounted below PCI₀ by a factor of 1/(1+λ/2). For λ = 0.10: discount = 1/1.05 ≈ 4.8%. This conservative discounting is by design — it ensures that no governance configuration receives a perfect RPCI = 1 unless λ = 0 and PCI₀ = 1, making the framework resistant to trivial perfect-score outcomes.

B.1 Formal Mathematical Specification
The following equations provide a complete formal definition of all computed quantities in the NLP-SVM pipeline. Equation numbering continues from the main text (Equations 1-10) for coherence.
B.1.1 TF-IDF Feature Weighting
The term frequency-inverse document frequency weight for term t in document d from corpus D is defined as:

tf-idf(t, d, D)  =  tf(t, d)  ×  log [ (1 + |D|) / (1 + df(t, D)) ]  +  1

where tf(t, d) = log(1 + count(t, d)) (sublinear smoothing), df(t, D) = number of documents containing term t, and |D| = corpus size. The ‘+1’ additive smoothing prevents zero-division on unseen terms. This formulation (scikit-learn default with sublinear_tf = True) was chosen over raw term frequency for three reasons: (i) it reduces the influence of high-frequency non-informative terms; (ii) it produces interpretable feature weights that can be directly inspected alongside the domain lexicons; and (iii) it avoids the vocabulary size and training data requirements of contextual embedding models such as BERT, which would be disproportionate for a specialised 87-document corpus. The limitations of this choice relative to transformer-based models are acknowledged in Section B.5.
B.1.2 Salience Dimension Scoring
Let V_dim denote the lexicon (valence term set) for dimension dim ∈ {P, L, U}, and let V_all = V_P ∪ V_L ∪ V_U denote the union of all valence terms. The raw salience score for document d on dimension dim is:

score_dim(d)  =  ∑_{t ∈ V_dim} ①(t ∈ tokens(d))  /  ∑_{t ∈ V_all} ①(t ∈ tokens(d))          (B.1)

where ①(·) is the indicator function. The denominator counts all valence-bearing tokens across all three lexicons present in d, ensuring scores are comparable across documents of varying length. The VADER compound sentiment adjustment applied to urgency is:

U_adjusted(d)  =  clip( score_U(d)  +  0.10 × compound(d),  0,  1 )          (B.2)
where compound(d) ∈ [-1, 1] is the VADER compound score (Hutto & Gilbert, 2014). The ceiling of ±0.10 was selected through sensitivity testing on a 20-document hold-out sample drawn from the corpus, comparing VADER-adjusted versus unadjusted urgency scores against annotator judgements. Adjustments exceeding 0.10 produced no improvement in annotator agreement and inflated urgency scores for documents with incidentally positive sentiment.
B.1.3 Stakeholder Acceptance Score (S_score)
Let G = {government, community, investor, NGO, labour} be the set of stakeholder groups, D_g ⊆ D the subset of documents attributed to group g, and pʲ(d) the SVM-predicted probability of pro-integration stance for document d. The group-level acceptance score is:

acc(g)  =  (1 / |D_g|) ×  ∑_{d ∈ D_g}  pʲ(d)          (B.3)

The S_score is then the SIC-weighted mean across stakeholder groups:

S_score  =  ∑_{g ∈ G}  SIC_g × acc(g)  /  ∑_{g ∈ G}  SIC_g          (B.4)

where SIC_g is the Stakeholder Influence Coefficient for group g (Equation 2, main text; Marikana values: SIC_community = 0.749, SIC_investor = 0.770, SIC_labour = 0.807, SIC_government = 0.703, SIC_NGO = 0.686). The PCI is then computed per Equation (1) of the main text:

PCI  =  w₁ × I_score  +  w₂ × R_score  +  w₃ × S_score          (Eq. 1, main text)

with w₁ = 0.30, w₂ = 0.35, w₃ = 0.35 for the Marikana case. The robustness-adjusted RPCI (Equations 5–8, main text) applies a variance penalty λ = 0.10 on the weighted standard deviation of domain scores.

B.5.1 Scalability to Larger Corpora
For deployment at continental scale (e.g., multi-country SADC/ECOWAS monitoring), three adaptations are recommended. First, the SVM may be replaced with a linear kernel (LinearSVC) for corpora exceeding n = 5,000 documents, reducing training complexity to O(n × V) while preserving comparable classification performance on high-dimensional TF-IDF features. Second, TF-IDF vectorisation supports incremental updates via HashingVectorizer, enabling streaming document ingestion without full corpus refit. Third, for corpora requiring multilingual processing (isiZulu, Sesotho, French-language Francophone documents), language-specific tokenisation models and translated lexicons are required; this constitutes a priority direction for future development noted in Section 5.2 of the main text.
B.5.2 Limitations of the TF-IDF and SVM Approach
The current implementation relies on TF-IDF bag-of-words features and a radial basis function SVM classifier. This constitutes an established but relatively standard machine learning pipeline rather than state-of-the-art natural language processing. Three specific limitations merit acknowledgement. First, TF-IDF does not capture word order, negation, or contextual polarity beyond the VADER adjustment; the phrase 'rights were denied' is treated equivalently to 'rights were upheld.' Second, the RBF-SVM is not well-suited to very large corpora due to quadratic training complexity. Third, domain-adapted transformer models (e.g., BERT fine-tuned on mining governance corpora) would likely improve salience classification accuracy, particularly for nuanced legitimacy claims. The term 'AI-enhanced' in the paper title is therefore intended to distinguish SCIPRA from purely expert-driven MCDM, not to claim transformer-level AI sophistication. Benchmarking against BERT-based classifiers is identified as a priority direction for future work (Section 5.2, main text). It should further be noted that the cross-validation metrics reported in Table 6 of the main text (accuracy = 81.6%, F1 = 0.837) are derived from the same 87-document Marikana corpus used to construct and illustrate the case argument. These metrics therefore reflect in-sample classification performance within a single case context rather than out-of-sample generalisation to independent governance settings. Cross-case validation — applying the trained pipeline to an independently annotated corpus from a second African mining jurisdiction (e.g., Zambia’s Copperbelt or Ghana’s Obuasi) — constitutes a priority direction for future work and is explicitly identified as such in Section 5.2 of the main text.

C.9 Methodology Transparency Audit
Table C9.1 provides a structured audit of all 15 P, L, U scores, classifying each by subjectivity level, primary source, and reviewer verification pathway. A score is classified as ‘Low’ subjectivity when it is formula-derived or directly quantifiable from a primary source; ‘Low-Medium’ when a formula-derived base is adjusted by a single documented event whose magnitude involves calibrated judgement; and ‘Medium’ when the base score is set by theoretical anchoring with documented evidence support (as for Community Power).
Audit summary: Of the 15 P, L, U attribute scores, 8 (53%) are Low subjectivity (formula-driven or directly quantifiable), 6 (40%) are Low-Medium (formula-derived base with one calibrated adjustment), and 1 (7%) is Medium (Community Power, theoretically anchored with documented support). No score is classified as High subjectivity. The single Medium score (Community Power = 0.32) is the most theoretically sensitive; its ±0.05 sensitivity range [0.27, 0.37] does not materially alter the SIC ranking (community remains third-highest, SIC ∈ [0.724, 0.774] vs. baseline 0.749). This is consistent with the Monte Carlo finding that P(PCI ≥ 0.70) = 99.5% across 10,000 parameter combinations (Section 4.7, main text), confirming that the single Medium-subjectivity score does not threaten the framework’s conclusions.

Validation against observed outcomes: The P, L, U scores are externally validated by their consistency with the documented Marikana conflict dynamics. The stakeholder group with the largest Power–Legitimacy gap (Investor: 0.88 vs. 0.65, Δ=0.23) is empirically associated with the greatest governance failure (Farlam Commission attribution). The group with the lowest Power but highest Legitimacy+Urgency (Community: P=0.32, L=0.92, U=0.95) is the group whose exclusion is formally identified as the proximate cause of the conflict (Farlam Commission, 2015, p.487). This structural correspondence between the salience scores and the documented causal narrative provides external validity evidence independent of the SVM classification pipeline.
